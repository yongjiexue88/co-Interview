What Are The Z And T-Tests, And When Should You Use Each?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Here’s the deal: these tests do the same basic job comparing means, but they don’t play by the same rules. A Z-test assumes you actually know the population variance, or you’ve got a huge sample that makes the estimate stable.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">A t-test handles the messier scenario where you don’t know the variance and your sample size is on the small side. Think of it like this: if the dataset looks like it came from a college stats textbook, then use a Z-test. If it seems like something your product manager scraped together at 1 a.m., t-test.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Why Do They Ask This?</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">They’re trying to figure out if you actually understand assumptions, not whether you memorized a formula five years ago and haven’t questioned it since.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">How To Answer (No Nonsense Version)</h4><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Say which test you’re using and what your null hypothesis is.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Call out assumptions like a grownup: independence, normality (or “close enough”), known vs unknown variance.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Mention the test statistic and degrees of freedom without turning it into a TED Talk.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Explain how you’d decide: p-value vs alpha.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Add a quick note on what you’d do if the assumptions look sketchy. (t-tests are tough but not magical. Bootstrapping exists for a reason.)</li></ul><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Example Answer</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“I’d start by saying a Z-test works when the population standard deviation is known or the sample is big enough that the estimate barely wobbles. Then I’d write out the formula, keep it simple, and pick my alpha.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If sigma isn’t known and the sample is tiny, which is basically every real-world dataset ever, I’d switch to the t-test and mention the heavier tails and degrees of freedom. After that, I’d say I always check normality or at least sanity-check the residuals. If the data looks wild, I’ll use bootstrapping instead of pretending the assumptions magically hold.”</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">2. What Are The Drawbacks Of The Given Data Layouts, And How Would You Reformat Them For Analysis?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If you’ve ever opened a spreadsheet and instantly felt tired, that’s what this question is about. Wide student-score tables with repeat headers, mystery columns, and inconsistent types are a guaranteed way to make even a simple analysis feel like unpaid manual labor.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">What The Interviewer Actually Wants To See</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Whether you understand that “messy data” isn’t a vibe, it’s a productivity tax. They want to know if you can diagnose the issue and turn chaos into something you can query without wanting to quit tech.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">How To Answer</h4><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Point out the specific problems, not vague hand-waving.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Propose a proper long-format table with one row per observation.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Lay out how you’d transform it: unpivot/melt, convert types, dedupe.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Mention validation steps so they know you’re not breaking things.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Sprinkle in tools you’d actually use (pandas, SQL UNPIVOT, ETL steps).</li></ul><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Example Answer</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“I’d start by calling out the issues: repeat headers, scores split across 12 different columns, mixed types. Then I’d propose the basic long-format fix: student_id, exam_id, date, score, maybe a source_file tag so whoever comes after me knows where things came from.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Then I'd unpivot the mess, convert the scores to numeric, flag missing values, and run a quick count check to make sure I don’t accidentally drop half the data. Finally, I’d run a fast validation query, something like checking min/max scores per exam because nothing kills trust faster than silent data corruption.”</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">3. What Metrics Would You Use To Determine The Value Of Each Marketing Channel?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Everyone lists CAC, LTV, ROAS, conversion rate, etc., because that’s what Medium articles taught them. Interviewers know this. What they’re looking for is whether you understand that incrementality is the whole point. If you can’t tell what would’ve happened without the channel, the other metrics are just expensive decoration.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Why They Ask This</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">They want to see whether you can tie metrics to actual business value rather than just dumping out a glossary.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">How To Answer (No Fluff)</h4><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Pick a handful of real metrics: CAC, LTV, ROAS, conversion rate, ARPU.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Show you can compute them with real data (cohorts, transactions, spend).</li><li class="text-gray-300">Bring up incrementality testing because that’s where grownups live.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Segment by funnel stage or cohort so you’re not averaging garbage.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Talk about monitoring so channels don’t quietly decay for months.</li></ul><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Example Answer</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“I’d start with CAC = channel spend / new customers. That shows whether the channel actually moves the needle or if we’re paying for conversions that would’ve happened anyway.
