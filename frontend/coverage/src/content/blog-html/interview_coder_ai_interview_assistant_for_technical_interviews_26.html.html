The right questions show you care more than just getting the offer; you’re figuring out if you want to work there.<br><br>In this guide, I’ll share how I learned to ask questions that open up honest conversations about the codebase, the culture, and what growth looks like once you’re in. About the job, the team, the product. You’re not just speedrunning LeetCode and hoping for a lucky break. You’ve read the job post, maybe poked through their GitHub, and noticed they launched something new last month. Asking “What trade-offs did you deal with during that launch?” shows you did more than memorize a script.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Curiosity Isn’t Just a Buzzword: It’s How You Think in Public</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">The best candidates I’ve seen? They listen. Then they ask one good follow-up that makes the interviewer pause and go, “Oh yeah, that was tricky.” That’s the moment you win. Try asking things like “Can you walk me through a recent design debate, maybe between speed vs reliability how it played out?” Now you’ve got a real story, not a vague answer. Bonus, it shows you’re the type who follows threads, not just headlines.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Culture Isn’t Ping-Pong Tables: It’s How People Talk to Each Other</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Forget the perks. Ask how they handle disagreements.</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Ask, “How do you run design reviews?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">Ask, “What does mentorship actually look like for juniors?”</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You're not looking for the perfect answer. You’re listening for tone. If someone flinches when you mention feedback or gives some half-baked answer about “open-door policies,” trust your gut.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Don’t Walk Into a Burning Codebase Blind</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Please don’t be the person who joins a team and only then asks how bad the tech debt is. Ask up front:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">“How old is the codebase?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“How often do you ship?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“What does your <a href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/386135200_Continuous_Integration_and_Continuous_Deployment_A_Deep_Dive_into_Best_Practices" target="_self" class="text-white underline decoration-gray-400 underline-offset-2 hover:decoration-gray-600">Continuous Integration and Continuous Delivery</a> (CI/CD) look like, and how do you catch bugs before prod?”</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">These aren’t nitpicks, they’re your day-to-day. It’s your future weekends on-call. Ask like someone who’s going to inherit that pager.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Ask Smarter Questions: What I Actually Asked Before I Joined Amazon</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Look, most interview questions people ask are... boring. "What's the culture like?" Cool. You’ll learn more about the culture in your first Slack thread than from a recruiter’s canned response.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If you're serious about evaluating a job, ask like someone who gives a damn about doing the job. Here's how I’ve approached it every time I got past the phone screen, and yes, these kinds of questions helped me land at Amazon, Meta, and TikTok. <a href="https://www.interviewcoder.co/" target="_self" class="text-white underline decoration-gray-400 underline-offset-2 hover:decoration-gray-600">Interview Coder</a> made it easy to prep, but these questions? This is what helped me figure out whether I even wanted the offer.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Let’s break it down.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">What Success Looks Like (Before You Even Write a Line of Code)</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">One of the best things you can ask:</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“If I crush it in my first 90 days, what does that actually look like?”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Forget vague motivational nonsense. You want numbers. You want outcomes. Ask:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">What are the immediate priorities for this role?</li><li class="text-gray-300">What does ownership really look like here? Like, who decides when something ships?</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You’re not just trying to impress them; you’re testing if they know how to manage engineers properly.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Is Growth Just a Buzzword or Do They Actually Promote People?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This is where things get real.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Ask:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">“What are the promotion criteria?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“How often do people get feedback that actually leads to <a href="https://sg.indeed.com/career-advice/career-development/what-is-career-progression" target="_self" class="text-white underline decoration-gray-400 underline-offset-2 hover:decoration-gray-600">career progression</a>?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“Does the company cover learning budgets or conference trips?”</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If they squirm or give you a vague “we support growth,” run. That usually means: “you’ll be stuck on the same level for 3 years while your manager forgets you exist.”</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">What’s the Actual Work Like (Not the Shiny Job Post Version)</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">People love to sugarcoat this part. Don’t let them.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Ask:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">“How much of the week is spent building vs fixing things that break?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“How often do I get pulled into on-call rotations?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“Are sprint retros real or just calendar noise?”</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Your time is expensive. You deserve to know if you’re entering a death spiral of tech debt and pager alerts.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">When to Ask About Salary (Spoiler: Not Yet)</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Don’t lead with “how much does it pay?” That’s like asking someone to marry you before the first date.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Early interviews are about fit. If the vibe is good and you’re deep in the loop, you bring up pay, equity, and benefits.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">But it’s totally fine to ask about:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Remote work flexibility</li><li class="text-gray-300">Time off policies</li><li class="text-gray-300">Parental leave and other actual life stuff</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Keep the comp convo until you’ve got leverage.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Interview by Interview: What to Ask at Each Stage</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You don’t need to shotgun 20 questions. Just ask the right ones based on who you’re talking to:</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Recruiter Screen</h4><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">“What does the full interview loop look like?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“Who makes the final hiring call?”</li></ul><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Technical Round</h4><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">“What assumptions should I make about constraints?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“What matters more: speed or readability?”</li></ul><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Manager Round</h4><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">“What’s your biggest headache right now?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“What’s the highest-stakes project for this team this quarter?”</li></ul><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Peer Round</h4><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">“What tools do you actually use?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“How long does it take to get a PR reviewed?”</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You’re not there to be polite but to collect signals.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">My Favorite Questions (AKA Stuff That Actually Helped Me Decide)</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Here’s a grab bag I keep coming back to:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">What’s the tech stack, and who decided that?</li><li class="text-gray-300">How do you manage tradeoffs between roadmap and tech debt?</li><li class="text-gray-300">What happens when prod goes down? Who owns that mess?</li><li class="text-gray-300">How do PMs, engineers, and designers make calls together?</li><li class="text-gray-300">What’s onboarding like? Sink or swim, or is there structure?</li><li class="text-gray-300">How do you measure output without turning into Jira police?</li><li class="text-gray-300">How fast is your release cycle, and is it actually automated?</li><li class="text-gray-300">Tell me about the last thing that shipped and flopped.</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If they hesitate, that tells you more than the answer.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Pick Your Battles: You Only Need a Few Great Questions</h3><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Here’s The Move</h4><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Pick two categories that matter to you now (growth, compensation, day-to-day, etc.)</li><li class="text-gray-300">Prep two questions per interviewer. That’s it.</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Clean. Focused. Engineer</h2><div class="mb-8"><span data-media="image">[IMAGE 800x600]</span></div><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">1. Ask This Before They Ask You Anything</h3><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">“Why Is This Job Open?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Don’t wait until the end when they ask, " Do you have any questions for us?” That’s amateur hour. Ask this first.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You’ll learn everything you need to know in the first 30 seconds:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Are they growing? Backfilling? Losing people?</li><li class="text-gray-300">Is this a mess they need cleaned up?</li><li class="text-gray-300">Are you walking into chaos, or into a team that’s finally adding firepower?</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Whatever they say, that’s your blueprint. If they’re behind on shipping, you talk about how you’ve hit tight deadlines. If they lost a backend dev, you position yourself as the backend fixer. They tell you what they need, and you show them you are that.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You flip the dynamic from “I hope they like me” to “Let me show you I solve the problem you just told me about.”</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">2. Don’t Just Ask What You’ll Build: Ask What You’ll Build With</h3><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">“What Programming Languages, Frameworks, And Libraries Are You Using?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Too many folks skip this or treat it like small talk. Don’t. You’re about to spend months (maybe years) in that codebase. You'd better like the tools.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Ask why they picked those tools. Especially if they’re weird or unexpected, it shows you’re not just a code monkey, you think about tech stacks and tradeoffs.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Also, listen for legacy red flags. If they say, “We still use ColdFusion but we’re migrating to something modern soon,” run.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">3. Ask where the bodies are buried</h3><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">“What Kind Of Databases Do You Use, And Where’s The Data Stored?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Look, nobody puts “bad <a href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/computer-science/data-architecture" target="_self" class="text-white underline decoration-gray-400 underline-offset-2 hover:decoration-gray-600">data architecture</a>” on the job posting. But you should know that upfront if they’re still running production on an EC2 instance with a Postgres DB they SSH into.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Figure out:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">SQL, NoSQL, or a Frankenstein combo?</li><li class="text-gray-300">On-prem or cloud? (And which one: AWS, Azure, GCP, or mystery box?)</li><li class="text-gray-300">Are they secure? Or duct-taping stuff together and praying?</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If you’ve got experience with their stack, bring it up. If not, at least show you’re thinking about it.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">4. Check their Git Game</h3><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">“What Source Control Do You Use?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If they say anything other than Git, smile, nod, and quietly move on with your life.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">More importantly, do they have a real branching model? Do PRs get reviewed? Is there CI? Or is it YOLO pushing straight to the main?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This question helps determine whether you're walking into a team with engineering discipline or just vibes.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">5. Find Out How Much Tech Debt You’re Inheriting</h3><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">“Where’s Your Tech Debt? And Are You Actually Fixing It?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Every company has tech debt, and that’s fine. But are they fixing it or just ignoring it and hoping it goes away?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Listen for things like:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">“We don’t have tests for that part of the system.”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“We’re planning to rewrite that module someday.”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“The person who built that left, and we don’t really touch it anymore.”</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Red flags everywhere.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Suppose you’re the kind of dev who can clean up messes, cool. But don’t walk in blind.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">6. Ask Who You’ll Actually Be In The Trenches With</h3><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">“How Big Is The Team And Who’s On It?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This one tells you what your day-to-day looks like. Small team? You’re doing everything infra, frontend, deployments, maybe even support tickets.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Big team? You may be cranking out <a href="https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/api-endpoint" target="_self" class="text-white underline decoration-gray-400 underline-offset-2 hover:decoration-gray-600">API endpoints</a> all day.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Neither is wrong. Just make sure it fits you.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Also, do they have testers, designers, DevOps folks? Or are you expected to wear 14 hats and still ship on Fridays?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">7. Check How Often You’ll Get Interrupted</h3><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">“How Many Meetings Do Engineers Typically Attend Each Week?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You can be the most innovative developer on earth, but if you’re stuck in meetings five hours a day, you'll have trouble getting anything done.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Some teams run lean with 2–3 standups weekly, while others turn your calendar into a crime scene.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Ask early. Guard your deep work hours like your mental health depends on it. (Because it does.)</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">9. Estimation: Who Pulls the Strings?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I hate guessing how long things take without context. Worse than guessing, some PMs rewrite your estimate just to meet a deadline.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Ask how estimation works. Who’s involved? What method? What happens when the estimate doesn’t match reality?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If you’re not in the room when timelines are decided, prepare to be blamed when they slip.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">10. Code Reviews: Is It Feedback or Ego Theater?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I’ve seen code reviews that felt like a mentoring session. I’ve also seen code reviews that felt like hazing.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Ask:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Who does reviews?</li><li class="text-gray-300">What kind of feedback is normal?</li><li class="text-gray-300">Can juniors review seniors?</li><li class="text-gray-300">Is it async or live?</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">A culture of thoughtful code reviews = a culture where engineers actually grow. Anything else is noise.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">11. Local Testing + Deployment Cadence: How Painful Is Your Dev Loop?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You should be able to test code on your own machine. Sounds obvious, but I’ve joined projects where even that was impossible.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Ask:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Can I run the app locally without begging DevOps?</li><li class="text-gray-300">How soon does a pull request make it to prod?</li><li class="text-gray-300">What’s the deployment checklist?</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Fast loops make happy devs. Slow ones make Slack-fueled burnout machines.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">12. Releases: Do Your Commits Ever See Daylight?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Some teams “release” once a quarter, while others push to prod 15 times daily. I don’t care which one you choose; just make sure you know what you’re signing up for.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Also ask:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Is there a release manager?</li><li class="text-gray-300">How are rollbacks handled?</li><li class="text-gray-300">What’s the worst deploy they’ve had?</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This isn’t about getting dirt, it’s about understanding risk and responsibility.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">13. Process Drift: Are They Evolving or Stuck?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Every team either adapts or calcifies. Ask how their process has changed over the last year.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This one question tells you:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Whether they reflect</li><li class="text-gray-300">Whether they experiment</li><li class="text-gray-300">Whether they’re open to feedback (aka, whether your ideas will matter)</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I once joined a team that claimed to be " agile” but hadn’t changed anything since 2014. Pass.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">14. Dev’s Org Placement: Are You Heard or Herded?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Where does the engineering org live?</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Do developers report to a CTO or a marketing exec?</li><li class="text-gray-300">Is QA part of the team or a separate afterthought?</li><li class="text-gray-300">Are data, infra, and product all sitting at the same table?</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This isn’t politics. It’s power. Who owns the product vision? Who gets listened to? Who gets blamed?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If engineers aren’t in the loop early, you’re just a code monkey in a JIRA zoo.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">15. Planning &amp; Prioritization: Are Engineers Involved or Dictated To?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If you’ve ever built something that didn’t need to be built, you know how painful it is.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">So ask:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Who decides what gets built?</li><li class="text-gray-300">Do devs get a seat at the table during roadmap planning?</li><li class="text-gray-300">Has the team ever successfully prioritized tech debt?</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">The best teams I’ve worked with had engineers involved from the start, not after six managers and a finance guy had already signed off on the spec doc.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Ask how design and engineering decisions get made. Ask how disagreements are handled. Team vote? Tech lead calls the shot?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You’ll learn quickly if this is a group of adults or a hierarchy pretending to be one.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">16. Ask How They Measure “Good”</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Look, every company loves to say they care about quality. But how do they actually prove it?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I always ask this in interviews: “What metrics do you track for engineers?”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If they jump straight to “story points,” “velocity,” or “how fast we ship,” I raise an eyebrow. Fast is great, but fast and broken is just tech debt on steroids.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You want to hear things like:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Defect escape rate (how many bugs make it to prod)</li><li class="text-gray-300">Mean time to recovery</li><li class="text-gray-300">Code review turnaround time</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Those are signs they care about outcomes, not just output. They care about more than shipping for the sake of shipping.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">17. Ask when they last took a risk</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Here’s the question that always gets an awkward pause:</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“When’s the last time the team tried something new like a new language, framework, or weird idea and how did it go?”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If they haven’t tried anything in years, that tells you everything. That’s not a team that’s learning. That’s a team running on autopilot.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">And if they did try something, dig into how leadership reacted, especially if it flopped.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Did people get burned for failing?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Or did they treat it as part of the job?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I want to work somewhere that expects a few misfires. That’s where the real learning happens, not on teams where one bad pull request gets you sidelined.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">18. Ask If Managers Still Touch Code</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This one’s personal for me.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Early in my career, I had a manager reviewing my code who hadn’t written a line in six years. That was brutal. The feedback was vague and nitpicky, and usually missed the real issues.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">So now, I always ask:</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“Do any of the managers still code?”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If they don’t? That is no problem; just don’t expect technical mentorship.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">But if they’re giving feedback, they better be close enough to the codebase to provide feedback that helps you grow. Otherwise, it’s just noise.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white"><strong class="font-bold text-white">19. Ask How Often You’ll Talk To Non-Engineers</strong></h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Some engineers want deep focus. Others like bouncing ideas around across teams.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">So ask:</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“How much do engineers interact with other departments?”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Then ask why.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Is it because the team is collaborative? Or is the company just chaotic, and everyone ends up doing everything?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">On the other hand, if engineers are siloed, is this intentional so they can focus, or is it just poor communication?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I’ve been in both setups. Find what works best for you and see if the company matches.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">20. Ask How They Support Junior Devs: It Tells You Everything</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Even if you’re not junior anymore, this is one of my go-to questions:</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“How do you support junior developers on the team?”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Why? Because if the company invests in juniors, it usually means they invest in people. That’s the kind of culture that doesn’t ghost you when you’re stuck or blame you when something weird happens in prod.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Bad Answer</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“We expect them to keep up.”</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Good Answer</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“We pair them with a mentor and ramp them up with scoped tasks.”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This one’s a peek into how they build talent or whether they just burn through it.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">21. Ask What “Training” Actually Looks Like</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You’d be surprised how many companies say, “We train juniors,” and then just hand them Confluence links from 2017.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Ask this:</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“What does training look like for someone starting out?”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You’re not looking for some big fancy program; it's just a sign they’ve considered it.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Do they do code-alongs? <a href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/228653369_Pair_programming_When_and_why_it_works" target="_self" class="text-white underline decoration-gray-400 underline-offset-2 hover:decoration-gray-600">Pair programming</a>? Give juniors real tickets early on? They probably don't have a plan if they don’t have an answer.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">22. Ask What The First Week Of Work Looks Like</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This is a fun one because it breaks the script.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“What are the first few tasks you typically assign to new hires?”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If they say “setup your dev environment and then figure things out,” yikes.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You’re looking for answers like:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Small, scoped bugs</li><li class="text-gray-300">Internal tooling tasks</li><li class="text-gray-300">Shadowing a code review</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This tells you how intentional their onboarding process is and whether they just throw people into the fire or help them get their footing.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">23. Ask When Juniors Ship To Production</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This one tells you a lot about trust and team confidence.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“How soon do junior devs get to push changes to production?”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If the answer is “within the first month,” that’s a great sign. They trust their onboarding process and review pipeline and are not afraid to let people learn by doing.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If the answer is “not for a long time,” follow up with why.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Sometimes it’s for legit reasons. Other times, the culture treats juniors like liabilities instead of future rock stars.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">24. Ask If Mentorship Continues Beyond Onboarding</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Mentorship isn’t just a “first 30 days” thing.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">So I always ask:</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">“Is there ongoing mentorship after the first few weeks or months?”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Are juniors left to fend for themselves after onboarding ends? Or is there structured support, regular check-ins, pair sessions, and access to senior engineers who actually care?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This one matters a lot if you’re still early in your career. But it also matters if you’re mid or senior level. Because if you join, you might be the one mentoring, and you want to know what kind of culture you’re walking into.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">1. “How Did You End Up Here, And Why Are You Still Here?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This one’s gold. Ask it casually like you’re genuinely curious (because you should be). It’s not just about getting a feel-good story. It shows you care about people, not just perks. Listen to what they say and what they skip. That tells you everything.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">2. “Do You All Ever Run Hackathons?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If a team has hackathons, they give engineers space to think outside the box, build fast, and break stuff well. If they say “yes,” ask what came out of the last one. If it ended in pizza and a Jira ticket no one touched again, that’s one vibe. If something was shipped? That’s different.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">3. “Do Devs Actually Go To Conferences Or Meetups, Or Is That Just Marketing Talk?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I like to ask this one after they say they value learning. If no team member has been to a meetup in two years and is allergic to travel budgets, you know where things stand. Bonus points if they name specific conferences and devs who went.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Also, come ready. Mention a few events you’d want to attend. Signal you're not just asking, you're someone who cares about growing.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">4. “What Do Your Customers Actually Use The Product For?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If the person you’re talking to can’t answer this without reading off the website, that’s a flag. Ask what makes customers stick around. Sound engineers ask about code. Great ones care about why that code exists.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">5. “What’s The Biggest Thing The Company’s Trying To Do This Year?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Forget vision decks and brand manifestos. Ask what they’re actually trying to pull off this quarter or year. If they can tie your role directly into that effort, it's nice you’re not joining some backwater team that ships once a year. You’re in the flow.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">6. “How Have You Seen [Insert Company Value] Show Up In Real Life?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Every company has a list of values that sounds like ChatGPT generated them. This question cuts through that. You want the messy examples. The hard calls. Like: “We almost launched that thing, but didn’t because it went against XYZ.” Absolute values cost something. Look for receipts.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">7. “What Makes You Excited About Where The Company Is Heading?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">When someone answers this with genuine energy, not just “we’re growing” or “lots of opportunity,” pay attention. Do they light up talking about a new project, team, or direction? If they’re stoked, maybe you will be too.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">8. “What Are The Expectations For This Role In The First 30, 60, 90 Days?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Let’s skip the vibes and get practical. This question helps you determine whether they’ve considered the role. Some places will have a plan (excellent), while others will basically say, “I don't know, figure it out” (less significant). Expectations change over time, so ask about that, too.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">9. “Can You Walk Me Through The Team I’d Be Working With?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Get names. Get roles. Get a sense of size, structure, and chaos levels. Ask how they work together, do they pair? Do they throw tickets over the wall? You’ll spend much time with these folks, so you might as well know what you’re walking into.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">10. “What Tech Stack Are Y’all Using?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You’re not asking this to quiz them, you’re trying to see if you’ll be productive on day one or googling setup instructions on some deprecated internal Confluence wiki. Ask what tools they like, what they hate, and what’s being replaced soon.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">11. “How Does The Team Usually Plan Projects?”</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This one tells you more than you think. Do they run on sprints? Do they ship weekly? Is it chaos with deadlines that change on Slack every Friday? You want to know how work gets from idea → done. If their planning process makes sense to you, great. If not, at least you know.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Want to add a line about <a href="https://www.interviewcoder.co/" target="_self" class="text-white underline decoration-gray-400 underline-offset-2 hover:decoration-gray-600">Interview Coder</a> here to tie this in?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Let me know if you want these structured into a single blog section with markdown formatting. I'd be happy to format it blog-ready.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">25. “How Did You End Up Here: And What’s Kept You From Bouncing?”</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Forget the job description. This question gets to the real stuff. People don’t stay at a company for five years because of the snack bar. Ask the hiring manager why they joined and what’s made them stick around. Watch their face. You’ll learn more from their tone than the actual words.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If they say something generic like “the people,” push a little. “What about them?” You’re not interrogating, you’re looking for something real.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">26. “Ever Run A Hackathon Here?”</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This is how I find out if a company respects devs who like to mess around and build cool stuff outside the Jira hamster wheel. Hackathons don’t have to be fancy. I just want to know if they make space for engineers to be curious.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If they say yes, ask, “Did anything actually ship from it?” There’s a big difference between a hackathon that ends with pizza and one that ends with a feature.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">27. “Do People Here Actually Go To Meetups Or Conferences Or Is That Just Brochure Talk?”</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You want to join an alive team, not just cranking tickets in silence. Ask if developers attend events. Ask if they come back and share ideas. That tells you whether the team has a learning culture or just likes to say it does.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I usually have a few conferences in mind when I ask this. You should, too; it flips the dynamic. You’re not asking for permission but showing you care about leveling up.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">28. “What Do Your Customers Actually Use The Product For?”</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This is a sneaky-good question. You’re not just trying to impress them with business curiosity. You’re trying to understand how close engineering is to the people using what you build.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If they can’t give you a clear answer or, worse, they say, “That’s more of a product question,” you’ve got your signal.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">29. “What’s The Company’s Biggest Priority Right Now?”</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I ask this in every final round. It tells you whether leadership is focused or flailing. You’ll also get a glimpse of whether your work will matter or if you’ll just be in a corner shipping “nice-to-haves.”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">And can they tie the priority back to your potential role? That’s when you know you’re not being hired just to plug a hole.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">30. “How Have You Seen (Insert Company Value) Show Up In Real Life?”</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Every company’s a page that says, “We care about integrity and transparency.” Cool. Ask for a moment where that happened, especially when it would’ve been easier not to.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If they hesitate or if they give you some vague PR story, that tells you a lot. Values don’t mean anything unless they cost something.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">31. “What Are You Personally Excited About For The Future Here?”</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I love this one. It’s not “Where do you see the company in 5 years?” It’s “What gets you hyped?” You’ll get a sense of whether people are sticking around for a paycheck or because they like the direction of things.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Look for eye sparkles. Seriously. If they light up, that’s a good sign. If they say “lots of opportunity” and trail off... not as much.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">32. “What Are Your Expectations For This Role In The First 30, 60, And 90 Days?”</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If you don’t ask this, you’re setting yourself up for surprises. Some companies want you to ship in week one, while others want to marinate for a month. You need to know what kind of runway you’ve got.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Also, ask how those expectations shift over time. What looks good at 90 days? What looks great at 6 months? Their answers will tell you how much they’ve thought this through or if they’re just winging it.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">33. “Can You Give Me A Quick Snapshot Of The Team I’d Be Working With?”</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You’re not trying to memorize the org chart; you just want a vibe check. Who’s on the team? What do they do? How do they work together? Do they actually talk to each other, or is Slack silent?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This question helps you picture your day-to-day personalities, dynamics, and chaos. It's helpful if you’ve ever joined a team and realized on day two, “Oh no. I’m the only one who talks.”</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">34. “What Tech Stack Does The Team Use And What’s Being Phased Out?”</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">The stack tells you how modern (or not) the team is. But it’s the transition tech that tells you how honest they are.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Every company says it’s moving to TypeScript. Ask what’s still in PHP. Ask what’s still manual. You’re not judging; you’re gauging the mess you’re walking into.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">35. “How Do Projects Actually Get Planned And Shipped Here?”</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">No team says, “We’re disorganized and nobody knows what’s going on.” So don’t ask “Do you use agile?” Ask: “How does a feature go from idea to done?”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Do PMs toss specs over the wall? Do engineers push back on timelines? Do you have to write a doc for every one-liner change? Their answer tells you if you’ll be productive or stuck in planning purgatory.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">36. How Do You Know When Someone’s Doing A Good Job Here?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I once asked this after a final round at Amazon. The VP paused and said, “No one’s asked me that today.” That told me everything.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Asking about performance metrics shows you’re not there to coast, but you also want clarity. Will output measure your work? Code quality? Ship speed? Make them define it.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">37. Who Does This Role Report To? Can I Meet Them?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Your manager is half the job. I’ve had good ones and... some I’d rather not talk about publicly. Ask this early. If the hiring manager dodges the question or won’t introduce you, that’s a yellow flag.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">38. What’s Been Tough For Your Engineering Team Lately?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This is my go-to “real talk” question. No company is perfect. I’ve heard everything from “tech debt from 2016” to “cross-team drama we’re still figuring out.” Useful intel.
