I’ve coached folks who spent months grinding LeetCode and still flopped at round one. I’ve also seen people with below-average code speed crush their loops because they played the process right. You don’t need to be perfect. You need to be prepared in the right direction.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Here’s precisely how I approached interview prep when I landed at Amazon, Meta, and TikTok, and what I still tell people today.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Get Shortlisted: Make Your Resume Worth Picking Up</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Your resume is either a conversation starter or a one-way ticket to the archive.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Forget fluff like “motivated team player with a passion for learning.” Nobody’s calling you for that. Instead:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Start bullets with real verbs. Built. Shipped. Reduced. Designed.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Include real numbers. “Cut <a href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/engineering/application-programming-interface" target="_self" class="text-white underline decoration-gray-400 underline-offset-2 hover:decoration-gray-600">application programming interface (API)</a> latency by 40% for 2M+ users” is clear. “Improved performance” is not.</li><li class="text-gray-300">One line per bullet. Don’t write an essay.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Ditch anything older than 3 years unless it’s famous or weirdly fabulous.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Mirror a few keywords from the job description, but don’t go full keyword soup.</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Side projects? Keep it short. Stack used + one result. That’s it.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If you’re getting ghosted, run your resume past someone who’s gotten offers. Or do what I did: print 10 copies, give them to friends, and ask them where they stopped reading.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Know the Format Before You Train for It</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Before you touch a practice question, get this from the recruiter:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">What stages are in the loop?</li><li class="text-gray-300">What does each one actually test?</li><li class="text-gray-300">Is it collaborative? Timed? Open book?</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Most people guess and prep wrong. I ask straight up, “Is the coding round whiteboard or CoderPad?” Saves me days.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">The “Are You Even Technical?” Quiz Filter</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">These come in hot and fast:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Time complexity of bubble sort?</li><li class="text-gray-300">How many bits are in an int?</li><li class="text-gray-300">What does XOR do again?</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">They’re checking if you know CS basics or if you just play with React all day. I kept flashcards on Big O, binary ops, and JS quirks on my phone and scrolled through them while waiting in lines.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Online Coding Assessment = Write Code Fast Without Screwing Up</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Usually HackerRank or something similar. Rules matter here. Some real tips:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Learn how they handle stdin/stdout; it sounds dumb, but it can cost you.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Simulate the real deal: no Stack Overflow, timer running, weird I/O rules.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Practice test cases with trash input: empty arrays, weird types, duplicates.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Know how to debug without a debugger. If it crashes, you’re not getting partial credit.</li></ul><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Take Home Assignment: Your Mini Portfolio</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">These show up more with startups or small teams. They’re looking for taste, not just brute force.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">How I treat these:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Clean repo. A README that explains trade-offs.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Tests. Not 100%, but enough to show you’re not winging it.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Mention how you’d scale it (briefly).</li><li class="text-gray-300">Don’t ship it half-baked; it’s basically your portfolio in disguise.</li></ul><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Phone Screen: Can You Code and Talk at the Same Time?</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Most phone screens use a shared editor. Sometimes you can run code, and sometimes you can't.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">What I do:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Ask questions before writing anything. Show you’re not just a keyboard monkey.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Sketch out a plan and complexity before typing.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Talk while typing. Yes, it’s awkward. Still do it.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Practice in Google Docs or Notepad with no syntax highlighting.</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Frontend roles? Sometimes you get a CodePen-like tool. Make sure you’ve touched one before the call.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Onsite: Depth Check + Culture Fit Speedrun</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Usually 3–5 rounds: coding, design, and some behavioral stuff that feels like therapy.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Tips:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">If allowed, bring your laptop and preload your snippet library.</li><li class="text-gray-300">System design? Whiteboard first. If it gets serious, switch to diagrams or markdown.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Behavioral rounds aren’t just for HR. Don’t say “I work too hard” when asked about your weaknesses. Show actual stories with tension and outcome.</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Lunch chats? Ask how their last release went or what broke recently. You’ll get better signals than any Glassdoor review.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Pick One Language You Actually Use</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Stick to a language you write without Googling “how to sort an array in X.”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I used Python, but not because someone told me to, because I already used it for side projects. Whatever you pick:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Know the stdlib.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Know how to read files and parse JSON.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Know how to fake a HashMap from scratch.</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Do not cram a new language at the last minute because you think it’ll make you seem smarter. It won’t.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Practice With Purpose, Not Panic</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Solving 500 random LeetCode questions is a great way to waste your time.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Instead, run a clean loop every time you sit down:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Clarify the question and constraints.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Write the brute-force solution.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Try improving it.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Explain complexity.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Code clean.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Test with edge cases.</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Mix timed sessions with untimed review. Alternate typing with whiteboard practice. Do mock interviews, record yourself, cringe at the playback, and fix your gaps.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">That’s how I prepped, not by grinding, but by practicing the right stuff in the right way.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">A Study Plan That Doesn’t Fall Apart After Week Two</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If you’re working a full-time job or studying, don’t pretend you’re grinding LeetCode 6 hours a day. Set 11 hours a week. That’s doable. Break it down to 2–3 hours a day, 4 days a week. That’s your real window.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Here’s what worked for me:</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Weeks 1–4</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Arrays, strings, and hash maps. Two pointers. Sliding window. Recursion. These are your warmup reps. You'll see them everywhere.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Weeks 5–8</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Trees, graphs, BFS/DFS, heaps, basic sorting, intro DP.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Weeks 9–12</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">DP that makes you sweat. Graphs that ruin your weekend. Mixed sets to simulate the actual interview.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Start mock interviews by week 4 once you’ve got muscle memory for patterns. Don’t wait until you feel “ready.” You won’t.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Use spaced repetition for concepts and notes. You’ll forget what a min-heap does if you don’t revisit it. If you’re short on time, skip the fluff and do a curated list like NeetCode 75 but actually track by pattern, not problem name. That’s how you recognize them under pressure.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">The Tools That Actually Help (No Chrome Tabs Needed)</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You don’t need 15 platforms. You need discipline.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">LeetCode</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Most realistic interview problems. Stick to it.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">HackerRank</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Great if you keep running into input/output issues.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">CodeForces</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Optional. Only if you want to sharpen your speed and reflexes.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">CoderPad / CodePen</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Use this for mock interviews. Simulates real IDEs better than any tutorial.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Every time you screw up a problem, log it. Not just the mistake written down:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">What you tried</li><li class="text-gray-300">The core trick</li><li class="text-gray-300">Other ways to solve it</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Then read that log once a week. It’s your personal “how not to fail again” manual.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">System Design: Where Most People BS Their Way Through</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This part separates the decent engineers from the ones who get the big offers.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">It’s not about buzzwords. It’s about <strong class="font-bold text-white">trade-offs</strong> and <strong class="font-bold text-white">clear thinking</strong>.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Here’s the method I used:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Ask good questions. Force clarity on constraints.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Figure out scale: traffic, storage, reads/writes per second.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Draw basic components (no need for Picasso).</li><li class="text-gray-300">Pick data models. Talk through consistency and availability.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Walk through bottlenecks and failure scenarios.</li></ul><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Start With Classics</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed"><a href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/computer-science/uniform-resource-locator" target="_self" class="text-white underline decoration-gray-400 underline-offset-2 hover:decoration-gray-600">Uniform resource locator (URL)</a> shorteners, chat apps, news feeds, file storage, and video streaming.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You don’t need to memorize diagrams; you need to understand why each piece exists and when you’d swap it out.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">System Design Study Stack That’s Actually Worth It</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If you're gonna spend time, spend it right.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">ByteByteGo</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Best visuals, clean breakdowns of real systems.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Grokking System Design</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Basic, readable. Solid for juniors.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Exponent</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Video-based, interview-style examples. Easy to digest.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Advanced Grokking</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Go here once you’re past the basics and want senior-level prep.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Watch → Sketch → Talk it out → Repeat. Do mock design sessions with feedback. Don’t just “study.” Practice explaining.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Behavioral Rounds: Don’t Wing It, Write It</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Everyone thinks they can talk about teamwork and growth until they blank out or ramble for 10 minutes.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Use STAR</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Situation, Task, Action, Result. Simple, boring, but it works.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Focus on:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">What you did (not “we”)</li><li class="text-gray-300">What decision did you make</li><li class="text-gray-300">What went wrong and what changed after</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Your stories should feel tight. 3–5 minutes. Max.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">And don’t sugarcoat your failures, just show that you learned and actually applied it later.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Story Bank for Behavioral Prep (Don't Just Rehearse One)</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Hiring managers rotate the same 10 questions. Be ready.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Here are a few they’ll probably ask:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">“Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a teammate.”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“Describe a hard technical decision you made.”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“Walk me through a project you led. What changed because of it?”</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Write out 8–12 solid stories that hit common themes:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Ownership</li><li class="text-gray-300">Problem-solving</li><li class="text-gray-300">Communication</li><li class="text-gray-300">Learning something hard</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Then rehearse them with someone who won’t let you BS your way through. Get feedback. Tighten the ending.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">You Don’t Need to Be a Negotiation Ninja</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">But you do need to ask.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Prep your numbers:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Base salary range for the company and level</li><li class="text-gray-300">Typical equity and signing bonus (Blind or Levels. fyi helps)</li><li class="text-gray-300">Cost of living if you’re relocating</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">When you get an offer:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Separate the pieces (base, equity, perks)</li><li class="text-gray-300">Explain what you’re aiming for</li><li class="text-gray-300">Ask clearly and confidently, no need to apologize</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This isn’t about being greedy. It’s about being informed.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Practice Like It’s a Real Offer Call</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I used to freeze up the second comp talks came up. “What are your expectations?” felt scarier than any Leetcode hard. What helped? Reps. I’d bug a friend, “Pretend you’re the recruiter. Lowball me.” Then we’d go back and forth until I didn’t flinch.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Know how their leveling works. Know what L4 vs L5 means in that org, not just in your head. Dig through Levels. Fyi or Blind not to compare, but to prepare. Equity math? Don’t just copy the total number, do the mental gymnastics: What’s the strike price? What’s the vesting? What happens if the company stalls or exits early?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">And if you need time? Ask. Always ask. “Can I get back to you by Friday?” buys you time to talk to someone smarter or line up a backup offer. No one remembers who answered first. They remember who negotiated like they’d done it before.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Don’t Be Weird in Tech Interviews</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Here’s the cheat sheet I wish someone had slapped on my monitor during my first onsite:</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Do</h4><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Ask “dumb” questions early. Saves pain later</li><li class="text-gray-300">Say what you’re thinking as you go. Out loud. Yes, really.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Write code that’s boring and works. Bonus points if it’s readable.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Mention tradeoffs without sounding like a textbook.</li></ul><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Don’t</h4><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Sit in silence when stuck. Shrink the problem and keep talking.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Get cute with tricks. Just solve it.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Ignore hints. If they’re talking, they want you to succeed.</li></ul><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Don’t Skip the Night-Before Stuff</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I treat the night before like pre-fight rituals. Same hoodie. Same playlist. One warm-up problem I’ve done 10 times before is missing. No surprises.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Tech setup? Boring is good. Open Zoom early. Mic check. Cam check. No Chrome tabs with distractions.Breathe. Literally. Like box breathing if that’s your thing. Talk out loud while solving, even if it feels silly. You’re training your brain to think with an audience.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">After the interview? Write stuff down, even if it sucked, especially if it sucked. You’ll forget the pain. Don’t forget the lesson.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Build a Weekly Practice Schedule You’ll Actually Stick To</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Forget motivation. Build a schedule that runs on autopilot. Mine looked like this when I was grinding:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">4 coding sessions/week</li><li class="text-gray-300">1 system design session</li><li class="text-gray-300">1 behavioral prep</li><li class="text-gray-300">1 full mock interview</li><li class="text-gray-300">Flashcards daily.</li><li class="text-gray-300">Missed problems? Revisit them 3 days later, then 1 week, then 2. (Yes, that spaced repetition thing works.)</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Don’t just track hours. Track wins. Did a mock? Fixed a dumb bug? Explained recursion to your dog, and does it make sense? When I felt nervous, I’d simplify and pick one thing I knew I could show clearly. Not everything. Just one. saved me from blanking mid-sentence</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I used to go into interviews thinking, “I’ve solved 200 LeetCode problems, I got this.” But the moment I had to They’re about getting used to the pressure, thinking out loud without falling apart. Every time you rehearse that, your brain gets less jumpy. Your mouth stops lagging. Your answers stop rambling. It’s not magic. It’s reps.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If you’re still freezing during interviews, this is where I’d start.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">Make it feel like a real interview (no fake vibe)</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Pick questions that match the job you’re aiming for. Backend? You need DS/Algos, system design, and maybe concurrency. Frontend? DOM stuff, browser performance, mini UI tasks. SRE or platform? Think debugging logs and fire-fighting.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Use platforms that don't make you hate your life, LeetCode, CodeSignal, CoderPad, or just VSCode + screen share.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Add pressure. Set a timer. Clear your screen. Record everything. Talk like someone’s watching because eventually, they will be.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Time suggestions:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">30–45 min = Phone screen</li><li class="text-gray-300">45–60 min = Technical round</li><li class="text-gray-300">60–90 min = System design round</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">The goal isn’t to perform. It’s to practice performing.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">My Step-By-Step Guide for Running A Focused Mock Interview</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This is how I run mocks for myself, and for friends I’ve coached.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">1. Before Starting</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Send the job description, share your resume, agree on time limits, and double-check your setup. No excuses mid-call.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">2. Warm Up With Behavioral Stuff (5 Min)</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Just enough to settle nerves. “Tell me about a time…” blah blah. Treat it like stretching before deadlifts.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">3. Read The Prompt Out Loud</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Train yourself to actually read and clarify instead of speed-running to code.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">4. Plan First</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">No code yet. Talk about tradeoffs. Give a Big O guess. Draw the shape of the thing. Timebox 10 min.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">5. Pseudocode &gt; Code</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Write down function names or scaffolding. Don’t dive in with a blank file.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">6. Talk Through Small Tests</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Check edge cases, throw in weird inputs, and speak your thought process. Show you’re not just guessing.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">7. Hit Bugs? Narrate It</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">What broke, why, and how you fixed it. Interviewers care more about how you debug than if you make a typo.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">8. Wrap With A Summary</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Restate the solution, time/space tradeoffs, and what you’d fix next. Let them know the story isn’t over yet.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Mock interviews aren’t just drills. They’re scripts. Practice saying “Why I chose this” or “What if input size doubles?” until it sounds like second nature.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">What I review after each mock (and how I actually tracked progress)</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Finish your mock? Cool. Now write it down while it’s still fresh.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Here’s my quick log format:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">Date</li><li class="text-gray-300">Problem source</li><li class="text-gray-300">Time spent</li><li class="text-gray-300">Result (pass/fail/flop?)</li><li class="text-gray-300">2 things to fix</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Then I ask whoever played the interviewer:</p><ul class="mb-6 space-y-2 pl-6"><li class="text-gray-300">“Where did I lose clarity?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“What would you have asked next?”</li><li class="text-gray-300">“Which part of my code was messy?”</li></ul><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Don’t wait until the day before your Google screen to fix your feedback loop. You’re not a machine, but you can run better if you pay attention.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I used to track speed. Now I track clarity. Pick one to start with. You learn to make quick decisions instead of spiraling in your head.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">2. I spent way too long writing the perfect function and had zero time left to test. After a few more, I got ruthless about budgeting: 3 mins to think, 12 to build, 5 to clean and test. You don’t magically wake up knowing how to explain a trade-off clearly. All of that matters especially in final rounds when they're reading your energy, not just your code.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">5.
