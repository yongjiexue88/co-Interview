They’re watching how you think out loud under pressure. They want to know if you can choose the correct pattern and back it up with calm, clear reasoning, even if you're staring at a half-broken whiteboard in a room that smells like leftover pizza.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">I built Interview Coder to fix that part. Not to help you memorize bubble sort again, but to practice deciding between patterns when the clock’s ticking. I’ve used this exact list to prep for Meta, Amazon, and TikTok interviews, and I make every candidate I mentor start with it.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">So here it is. No magic. No fluff. Just the 15 patterns that actually come up with notes on how to talk through them when it counts.</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">1. Dynamic Programming</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You already know what this is, such as memoizing stuff to avoid repeating work. Most people think of it as some advanced technique, but it’s really just, “Did I already solve this? Then stop redoing it.”</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Used For</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Knapsack, coin change, and Fibonacci</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">What’s the state? What’s the recurrence? How are you caching?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">2. Tree Traversal</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">The tree’s not the hard part. The traversal orders are preorder, inorder, postorder, same visit, and different sequence.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Used For</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Reconstructing trees, symmetry checks, and recursive problems</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">What’s your base case? What’s the order? Stack space?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">3. Graph Traversal (BFS / DFS)</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You’ve done this 100 times. The real question is: when do you use <a href="https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Joseph-Iyanda-2/publication/370751322_Title_A_Comparative_Analysis_of_Breadth_First_Search_BFS_and_Depth_First_Search_DFS_Algorithms/links/64600949f43b8a29ba4c083c/Title-A-Comparative-Analysis-of-Breadth-First-Search-BFS-and-Depth-First-Search-DFS-Algorithms.pdf" target="_self" class="text-white underline decoration-gray-400 underline-offset-2 hover:decoration-gray-600">BFS vs DFS</a>?</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Used For</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Connected components, cycle detection, shortest path (unweighted)</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">How do you handle visited nodes? Why this traversal and not the other?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">4. Linear Search</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">If you bring this up in an interview, you'd better explain why you're not using something better.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Used For</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Small arrays, unsorted data, fundamental cases</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Why is brute-force acceptable here? What’s the tradeoff?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">5. Binary Search</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This is the one pattern you must be able to explain in your sleep. And still, most people screw up the loop condition.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Used For</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Sorted arrays, rotated arrays, weird search space tricks</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">What’s your invariant? What does your loop guarantee?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">6. Bubble Sort</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Not useful in practice, but it shows whether you understand what a swap is and when a sort can early exit.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Used For</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Toy problems, first-round warmups</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Why is it inefficient? When does it finish early?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">7. Insertion Sort</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">It looks dumb at first until you realize it crushes on almost-sorted input.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Used For</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Small input, hybrid sorts (e.g., TimSort)</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Why is it better than bubble? What’s the best-case behavior?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">8. Selection Sort</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Brute-force minimalism. Fewest swaps. Terrible on time, great on write-limited memory.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Used For</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Write-sensitive systems, sorting with constraints</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Why use this over insertion sort? What’s the pattern for selecting min?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">9. Hashing</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This is your shortcut pattern. If you ever need an O(1) lookup, your brain should immediately yell hash table.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Used For</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Frequency maps, memoization, uniqueness checks</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">What’s the hash function? How do you handle collisions?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">10. Depth-First Search (DFS)</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">You go deep first. Backtrack when stuck. People confuse it with recursion; it’s not the same thing.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Used For</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Backtracking, topological sort, path-finding</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">How are you marking visited? Are you pre/post-ordering?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">11. Breadth-First Search (BFS)</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">This is the shortest-path go-to when weights are equal. Always think of BFS when “fewest steps” shows up.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Used For</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Shortest path in a grid, level order traversal</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">What’s in the queue? Why does this guarantee a minimum steps?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">12. Divide and Conquer</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Split the problem, handle each piece, and merge them. Most people can code it. Very few can explain the cost correctly.</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Used For</p><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">mergesort, quicksort, <a href="https://www.monash.edu/student-academic-success/mathematics/matrices/matrix-multiplication-and-inverses" target="_self" class="text-white underline decoration-gray-400 underline-offset-2 hover:decoration-gray-600">matrix multiplication</a></p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">What’s your recurrence? What happens during combine?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">13. Merge Sort</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">It’s the boring but reliable sort. Predictable time. Extra space cost.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Used For</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">linked lists, stable sort needs, massive files</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Why not quicksort here? What’s the space complexity?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">14. Quick Sort</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Everyone loves this one until they forget to randomize the pivot and end up with the worst-case time.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Used For</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">In-place array sorts, general-purpose sorting</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">How are you choosing pivots? What’s the stack depth?</p><h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-6 text-white">15. Greedy Algorithms</h3><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Make the best move right now and hope it all works out. Sometimes it works beautifully. Sometimes it fails spectacularly.</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Used For</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Dijkstra, Huffman coding, interval scheduling</p><h4 class="text-lg font-bold mb-6 text-white">Talk Through</h4><p class="mb-6 text-gray-300 leading-relaxed">Why is greedy valid here?
